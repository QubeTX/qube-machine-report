# Design Framework

A streamlined design thinking process for crafting and refining solutions to problems —
products, systems, processes, experiences. Use when the user is *building* something, not
just choosing or diagnosing.

## How this framework uses the canvas

Design sessions are long-running and iterative — the canvas often outlives the chat
session. Treat it as a living design brief. By Step 5 (Prototype), the canvas should
already contain user insights, problem definition, requirements, research findings, and
chosen approach. The prototype refers to the canvas; the canvas doesn't try to summarize
the prototype.

---

## Step 1 — Empathize

Understand the user, their context, and their pain. This is not a formality. Designs that
skip empathy fail by solving the designer's problem rather than the user's.

### Sub-questions to ask

**1.1. Identify your stakeholders or users.**
- 1.1.1. Who will use, experience, or be affected by your design?
- 1.1.2. Who has this problem?
- 1.1.3. (If external users are involved) Why does this product need to exist?

**1.2. Understand their context and pain points.**
- 1.2.1. What specific struggles or frustrations do they face related to this design?
- 1.2.2. How are they currently dealing with these problems?
- 1.2.3. *"How might we achieve _____ for these users?"*

**1.3. Document key insights.**
- 1.3.1. What have you learned about your user so far?
- 1.3.2. What is the need or challenge from their perspective?
- 1.3.3. Why does it matter to them (and/or you)?

> **Designing for yourself:** When the user is designing for themselves, empathy is
> harder, not easier. They'll skip ahead to solutions that feel right rather than the ones
> they'd actually use. Force the empathize step anyway — what does your *future, tired,
> distracted self* need from this design?

---

## Step 2 — Define the Problem

Clarify the design challenge before generating concepts.

### Sub-questions to ask

**2.1. Clarify the design challenge.**
- 2.1.1. What is the problem? Where is the problem? Why does it matter?
- 2.1.2. What is the challenge? What needs to be improved?
- 2.1.3. What is the ultimate goal (or the outcome we aim to accomplish)?

**2.2. Determine requirements and constraints.**
- 2.2.1. What are the requirements for this project?
- 2.2.2. What are the limitations — budget, time, tools, materials, or other constraints?
- 2.2.3. Are there legal, ethical, or business considerations that apply?
- 2.2.4. What business problems will this solve?
- 2.2.5. Which features, performance metrics, or design criteria must be met?

**2.3. Consider feasibility.**
- 2.3.1. Do you have the resources, knowledge, and capacity to tackle this design?
- 2.3.2. Does it fit the necessary timeframe? Can you complete it within budget?
- 2.3.3. Does it align with your target user's or customer's needs?

> **Externalize at Step 2:** A **Force-Field Analysis** (`references/visual-models/comparison.md`)
> is often useful here — drivers vs. resistors of the proposed change. Helps surface
> constraints that aren't obvious until you map them.

---

## Step 3 — Research

Investigate before inventing.

### Sub-questions to ask

**3.1. Investigate existing solutions.**
- 3.1.1. Do current solutions exist that try to solve this problem?
- 3.1.2. Are these failing to meet the user's needs in any way?
- 3.1.3. Do these solutions offer niche-specific versions?
- 3.1.4. How much are people spending on similar solutions?
- 3.1.5. Can you use existing technologies — open-source or otherwise?

**3.2. Gather technical/practical knowledge.**
- 3.2.1. What questions do I have about how to build or structure this solution?
- 3.2.2. What observations can I make from user behavior or competitor analysis?
- 3.2.3. Which materials, software, or processes are available?
- 3.2.4. What is my solution limited by (cost, materials, complexity)?

**3.3. Analyze market and user needs.**
- 3.3.1. Are there untapped opportunities or a gap in existing solutions?
- 3.3.2. Do these insights affect how I define my problem or requirements?
- 3.3.3. Are there alternative approaches or new features suggested by my research?

---

## Step 4 — Ideate

**This is the divergent step.** Generate concepts. Push for breadth.

### Sub-questions to ask

**4.1. Generate multiple concepts.**
- 4.1.1. What are different ways to solve the problem?
- 4.1.2. How might we approach it if constraints (time, budget) were no issue?
- 4.1.3. What is the scope and timeframe for developing each possible solution?
- 4.1.4. Could we brainstorm the "worst possible idea" to spark creativity?

**4.2. Compare and narrow down ideas.**
- 4.2.1. Which solution is the most useful? Which is the least complicated?
- 4.2.2. Does any concept uniquely differentiate us?
- 4.2.3. Which ideas best fit our constraints, resources, or timeframe?

**4.3. Formulate a chosen approach.**
- 4.3.1. Which concept stands out, and why?
- 4.3.2. How will we develop this solution?
- 4.3.3. Does it clearly solve the main problem we identified?

> **Convergence/divergence move:** Step 4.1 is divergent — quantity over quality, no
> evaluation. Step 4.2 is convergent — narrow to a chosen approach. Communicate the
> mode shift explicitly. Apply the **Four Commandments of Divergent Thinking** from
> SKILL.md.

> **Externalize at Step 4:** A **Scenario Tree** (`references/visual-models/structure.md`)
> can be useful when concepts have branching consequences. A **Weighted Ranking** matrix
> (`references/visual-models/comparison.md`) is good for the 4.2 narrowing.

---

## Step 5 — Prototype

Build something rough. The point is to learn, not to ship.

### Sub-questions to ask

**5.1. Build a preliminary (low-fidelity) version.**
- 5.1.1. What materials do I need?
- 5.1.2. How do I build a functional model or early wireframe?
- 5.1.3. Could I quickly mock it on paper or with basic software?

**5.2. Document materials, dimensions, or specs.**
- 5.2.1. What are this model's unique characteristics or specifications?
- 5.2.2. How can I label or organize these details so I (or others) can replicate or
  refine the design?

**5.3. Seek quick feedback.**
- 5.3.1. Did it solve the problem or introduce new issues?
- 5.3.2. Was there anything unexpected about using or building this prototype?
- 5.3.3. What was my (or a tester's) first impression?

> **Step 5 is supposed to be embarrassing.** A good prototype is rough, fast, and
> obviously incomplete. Resist polish.

---

## Step 6 — Test

Define what success looks like, then measure against it.

### Sub-questions to ask

**6.1. Define test criteria and metrics.**
- 6.1.1. What does "working correctly" look like?
- 6.1.2. Did it accomplish what I wanted it to do?
- 6.1.3. Which specific tasks should users be able to complete efficiently?

**6.2. Conduct the tests.**
- 6.2.1. Did users struggle to complete any tasks?
- 6.2.2. Did anything happen that I didn't expect?
- 6.2.3. How should I gather feedback — via surveys, observation, or interviews?

**6.3. Analyze and interpret results.**
- 6.3.1. Did we meet our initial success criteria and constraints?
- 6.3.2. Did it truly solve the user's or stakeholder's problem?
- 6.3.3. Did they immediately understand how to use it?

> **Hypothesis testing move:** When you have multiple competing explanations for *why*
> a prototype isn't working, use the **Hypothesis Testing matrix**
> (`references/visual-models/causality.md`). Rank candidate explanations by which has the
> least inconsistent evidence — Popperian falsification.

---

## Step 7 — Refine

Synthesize feedback and iterate.

### Sub-questions to ask

**7.1. Synthesize feedback and revise.**
- 7.1.1. What are the strengths and weaknesses of this prototype?
- 7.1.2. How could we make it better? Is it practical, affordable, or efficient?
- 7.1.3. If major issues appear, do we need to revisit earlier steps?

**7.2. Validate improvements.**
- 7.2.1. Should I prototype again at a higher fidelity or keep it simple?
- 7.2.2. Does the revised version address the user's unmet needs better?
- 7.2.3. Are there new problems created by the changes?

**7.3. Prepare for release.**
- 7.3.1. Is this design working as best as it could?
- 7.3.2. Have I documented the final specifications and reasoning behind significant
  decisions?
- 7.3.3. Am I satisfied with usability, aesthetics, and functionality?

> **Step 7 should sometimes loop back to earlier steps.** If testing reveals the problem
> was misframed, you go back to Step 2, not forward to Step 8.

---

## Step 8 — Release

Ship, and plan for the post-ship feedback loop.

### Sub-questions to ask

**8.1. Final production and implementation.**
- 8.1.1. Will I mass-produce, or finalize the digital deployment?
- 8.1.2. Do I have a checklist to verify all deliverables are complete?

**8.2. Communicate results.**
- 8.2.1. How should I best communicate my results?
- 8.2.2. Should I make a drawing, report, or demonstration?
- 8.2.3. How do I ask for others' feedback on the final design?

**8.3. Gather post-launch feedback.**
- 8.3.1. Did users report issues completing any specific tasks?
- 8.3.2. Will others be able to use this solution equally well?
- 8.3.3. What improvements or next steps might be needed in future iterations?

---

## Closing this framework — sanity check

Before finalizing the canvas:

- Does the design actually solve the problem you defined in Step 2.1.1, or did the
  problem drift during prototyping?
- Are the unmet user needs from Step 1.2 actually addressed, or did some get quietly
  dropped?
- What's the most likely failure mode of this design once it's released? Is it
  acknowledged?
- If you handed this to a sharp colleague who hadn't been in the room, would they get it?

Confidence band on the design: based on how rigorously the prototype was tested vs. how
much depends on assumptions about user behavior.

---

## Facilitation notes for this framework

- **Don't skip Step 1.** Designs that skip empathy solve the wrong problem.
- **Don't fall in love with the first idea in Step 4.** Force breadth — at least 5
  concepts, including a "worst possible idea" — before narrowing.
- **For meta-design** (designing a process or skill rather than a product), the user
  *is* the user. Empathize with their future self.
- **Use the canvas to track unanswered questions across steps.** Designs accumulate open
  questions as they progress; the canvas's open-questions section is essential.
