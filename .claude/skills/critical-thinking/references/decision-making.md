# Decision-Making Framework

A structured process for making informed decisions when there are options to evaluate. Use
when the user is choosing between paths, comparing options, or needs to commit to a direction.

## How this framework uses the canvas

Decision-Making sessions accumulate facts, assumptions, constraints, options, and
evaluations across 7 steps. The canvas is essential here — by Step 5, the user is
evaluating options against criteria they wrote in Step 3, with constraints from Step 2.4,
under assumptions from Step 2.2. That's working-memory overflow without the canvas. Reach
for visual models early (see Step 5).

---

## Step 1 — Get Clarity

Make sure you're solving the right decision before evaluating options.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**1.1.** Define the decision you are making and describe its context.
- What exactly are you deciding?
- What's the situation surrounding this decision?

**1.2.** What are you looking to get out of this decision?
- What does success look like?

**1.3.** Does this decision have only one correct solution, or are there multiple options
that could meet your needs?
- Is this an "exact answer" decision or a "good enough" decision?

**1.4.** Take a minute to step back and reflect on this decision:
- 1.4.1. Is this the right thing to decide right now?
- 1.4.2. Is this a real problem?
- 1.4.3. Why do we need to make this decision?
- 1.4.4. What is the underlying need or desire here?
- 1.4.5. If I look at this situation differently, can I avoid making this decision entirely?

> **Reframe move:** Step 1.4.5 is the most powerful sub-question in the framework. Many
> "decisions" disappear when reframed. If the user is genuinely stuck, push hard on this
> one before continuing. Pair with the **Problem Restatement** techniques in
> `references/problem-solving.md` Step 1.6.

---

## Step 2 — Gather Information

Separate facts from assumptions, and identify what you still need to know.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**2.1.** List the facts that you know for sure about the situation.

**2.2.** What assumptions are you making, and how can you test them?
- 2.2.1. Is this thought illogical?
- 2.2.2. Is this thought extreme?
- 2.2.3. Is this thought inflexible?

**2.3.** Do you have all the information you need to decide?
- If not, can you get it?
- Or are there things that will simply remain uncertain?

**2.4.** When is the right time to make this decision?
- 2.4.1. Is the situation likely to change enough that it would be beneficial to wait?
- 2.4.2. When will you start losing opportunities if you don't decide?
- 2.4.3. Will there be consequences if you don't decide today?

**2.5.** Consider external inputs or perspectives.
- Whose opinions or expertise could provide valuable insight?

---

## Step 3 — Identify Constraints

Map the boundaries before generating options.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**3.1.** List all of your needs and wants within this decision.
- What is essential? What is unessential / nice-to-have?

**3.2.** List all the required resources to make and implement this decision.
- Money? Time? Energy?

**3.3.** What do you have control over in this decision?
- What do you *not* have control over?

**3.4.** What are the top 3 most important considerations when evaluating the options?

> **Canvas move:** capture the top 3 considerations as named criteria. They become the
> column headers in the weighted ranking matrix at Step 5.

---

## Step 4 — Generate Options

**This is the divergent step.** Push for breadth before any narrowing.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**4.1.** Brainstorm at least five options.
- Aim for as many as possible within 30 minutes.
- Include absurd and unconventional ideas — they often unlock the practical ones.

**4.2.** Use a random word as a starting point.
- Pick one and see where the idea leads. This breaks pattern-matching to obvious answers.

**4.3.** Is there any specific factor of this situation that could lead you to explore
new or different ideas?
- What's unusual about this situation that you haven't leveraged yet?

**4.4.** Rate each idea on a scale of 1 (useless) to 7 (superb).
- Review all ideas that received a 6 or 7.

> **Anti-anchoring move:** Don't let the user converge on the first 2 options. Push for
> at least five before any evaluation. Apply the **Four Commandments of Divergent Thinking**
> from SKILL.md.

---

## Step 5 — Evaluate Options

Break the decision into parts and work systematically. **This is where externalization
matters most.**

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**5.1.** Break down the decision into its most prominent parts.
- Make broader decisions first (eliminating the most options at once).
- Then make smaller decisions to narrow to a single choice.

**5.2.** For situations with only one correct solution: focus on quickly eliminating
dead ends.

**5.3.** For situations with multiple options that could work: decide whether you need
the *best* solution, or whether *any working* solution will meet your needs.

**5.4.** Once you have whittled your options to 3 or fewer, consider for each:
- 5.4.1. What are the objective features or facts related to these options?
- 5.4.2. How do I feel about each option?
- 5.4.3. What are each option's weaknesses, risks, or negatives?
- 5.4.4. What are the positives or benefits of each option?
- 5.4.5. What are the effects or consequences of choosing one option over the others?
- 5.4.6. How easily can I implement or obtain this option?
- 5.4.7. What does this option give me that no other option gives me?

### Externalize at Step 5

When you have 3+ options being weighed against multiple criteria, switch from prose to a
visual model. Pick based on the situation:

- **Pros-Cons-and-Fixes** (`references/visual-models/comparison.md`) — for each option,
  list pros, then cons, then *fix* the cons that can be fixed. Compare the unfixable cons.
- **Weighted Ranking matrix** (`references/visual-models/comparison.md`) — when criteria
  have unequal importance and need to be made explicit.
- **2×2 matrix** (`references/visual-models/comparison.md`) — when two dimensions dominate
  (effort vs. impact, urgency vs. importance, etc.).
- **Utility Matrix** (`references/visual-models/probability.md`) — when outcomes are
  uncertain and you need to weight by probability × value.

Render the chosen model as markdown in the working canvas.

> **Steel-man move:** Before finalizing, run the steel-manning routine on the *runner-up*
> option. If the strongest case for the runner-up is stronger than you initially gave it
> credit for, the decision isn't ready. For high-stakes decisions, use the formal
> **Devil's Advocacy** procedure in `references/devils-advocacy.md`.

---

## Step 6 — Make the Decision

Commit. Then plan implementation.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**6.1.** Select the option that best aligns with your goals, constraints, and evaluations:
- 6.1.1. Which option offers the most benefit while minimizing risks?
- 6.1.2. Which option is the most effective? Which is the most efficient?
- 6.1.3. Does this decision align with your values and priorities?

**6.2.** What steps are required to implement the decision?

**6.3.** How will you track the outcomes of the decision?

> **Confidence band check:** Tag the decision with a confidence band (high/medium/low/
> speculation) on the canvas. Decisions that depend heavily on uncertain assumptions
> should be marked as such, and the assumptions revisited explicitly when uncertainty
> resolves.

---

## Step 7 — Reflect on the Decision

Don't skip this. The reflection is how decision-making improves over time.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**7.1.** What worked well, and what could be improved?

**7.2.** Did you consider all relevant factors and perspectives?

**7.3.** What insights will help you make better decisions in the future?

**7.4.** Are there patterns in your decision-making that need adjustment?

> **When to ask Step 7:** Sometimes the answer isn't available until the decision plays
> out. If so, schedule the reflection — name a specific date when the user will revisit.
> Otherwise it never happens. The canvas's "spaced revisit" field exists for this.

---

## Closing this framework — sanity check

Before finalizing the canvas:

- Does the chosen option pass the gut test? If you'd be uncomfortable telling a trusted
  colleague this is what you picked, that's a signal — surface it.
- Does the conclusion follow from the evaluation, or did the framework rubber-stamp the
  user's pre-existing preference?
- Are the unfixable cons of the chosen option actually tolerable, or were they minimized?
- What would have to be true in 3 months for this decision to look obviously wrong? How
  would you know early?

Confidence band: based on how heavily the decision rests on assumptions vs. facts.
Heavy-on-assumptions decisions are **medium** or **low** even when they feel certain.

---

## Facilitation notes for this framework

- **Don't skip Step 1.4.5** ("Can I avoid this decision?"). Many decisions evaporate there.
- **Don't skip Step 4.** Users will jump from "I have a problem" to "here are my two
  options." Force at least five.
- **Don't skip Step 7.** Even if the decision plays out over months, name the
  reflection date.
- **Recommend mode:** when the user has a documented brief and wants a recommendation
  fast, run Steps 1–3 silently from the inputs and present a recommendation at Step 5/6.
  But name what you skipped, so the user can object.
