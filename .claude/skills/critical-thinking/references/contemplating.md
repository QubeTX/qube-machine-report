# Contemplating With Wisdom and Joy

A personal and imperfect process for finding peaceful options in challenging situations. Use
when the user is facing overwhelming, uncertain, or emotionally charged situations where
perfectionism or anxiety might block progress.

This system is incomplete, imperfect, and fallible. Using it gets the user closer to living
a life aligned with wisdom, joyfulness, and peace — not toward a perfect answer.

## Core principles

Share these early. They set the tone.

- It is impossible to know the purest answer with certainty; often, there is no single
  correct answer.
- You are limited to the context of your time, space, and the information available to you.
- You can only do your best to do the next most necessary and right thing.

## How this framework uses the canvas

Contemplating sessions tend to be private and emotionally weighted. The canvas is for the
user alone, not for sharing. Keep the language personal. Capture facts and assumptions on
the canvas, but capture *feelings* in the user's own words — don't translate them into
analytical language.

---

## Step 1 — Establish Clarity

Separate facts from assumptions. Surface how emotions are coloring the picture.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**1.1. Write down the facts of the situation.**
- What is known or proven to be accurate and certain?
- Include the things that are more than likely going to happen.

**1.2. Identify the assumptions related to the situation.**
- What have you accepted as true but don't have proof of being certain?
- Include things that are less than likely to happen, such as extreme behavior or extreme
  situations.

**1.3. Clarify the emotional responses tied to the facts and assumptions.**
- How does each fact make you feel?
- How do your assumptions make you feel?

**1.4. Evaluate whether emotions are influencing assumptions or judgments.**
- Example: *"Is fear of failure causing me to assume that no jobs are available?"*
- Where might fear, hope, or attachment be distorting what you've labeled as fact?

---

## Step 2 — Gain Perspective

Broaden the view by involving others and challenging assumptions.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**2.1. Involve those directly impacted by the decision.**
A useful model: who would have to *sacrifice something* if things go poorly? Those people
are directly impacted. People who could be involved but wouldn't be sacrificing anything
are indirectly impacted. The directly impacted are the ones whose input matters most.

- 2.1.1. Have you shared the facts, assumptions, and emotions with those affected (such as
  a partner, co-founder, or close colleague)?
- 2.1.2. Have you really listened to them — for their facts, assumptions, and feelings,
  not just to confirm yours?

**2.2. Discuss and challenge assumptions.**
- For each assumption: *"How do I know this is true?"*
- Is this based on reality or on personal interpretation?

**2.3. Conduct light research and gather outside perspectives.**
- 2.3.1. Whose advice or expertise would be relevant here?
- 2.3.2. How will you filter advice based on its relevance to your specific context?

**2.4. Reflect on what you can appreciate in the current situation.**
- 2.4.1. What opportunities or resources are available right now?
- 2.4.2. What can you be grateful for in this situation?

---

## Step 3 — Acceptance

Help the user accept what they can't control and focus on what they can.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**3.1. Reflect on the opportunities available within current circumstances.**
- What can you build upon or utilize in what you already have?

**3.2. Accept the aspects of the situation you cannot control.**
- Example: *"Accept the uncertainty in job availability."*
- Name what cannot be changed. Then let it sit, instead of fighting it.

**3.3. Acknowledge that you cannot achieve everything at once.**
- Where are you holding yourself to a standard of doing it all, perfectly, now?
- What does incremental progress look like instead of perfection?

---

## Step 4 — Serenity Timing

Evaluate whether action is needed now or whether waiting is more beneficial.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**4.1. Evaluate the timeliness of the decision.**
- How urgent is this actually?
- Is action necessary now, or later?

**4.2. Decide if there is enough information to act, or if waiting is more beneficial.**
- What are the benefits of acting now? What are the drawbacks?
- What are the benefits of waiting? What are the drawbacks?

**4.3. Recognize areas where further preparation is causing procrastination.**
- Is "needing more information" actually masking fear of failure or fear of imperfection?
- What would you do right now if you had to decide today?

---

## Step 5 — Define "Enough"

Define what peace looks like and what counts as sufficient progress.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**5.1. Reflect on what peace looks like in the situation.**
- What is the minimum disruption required to reach a resolution or meet your goal?

**5.2. Evaluate what it means to have done "enough" for the day or the week.**
- What does incremental progress look like?
- Where can you trust that consistent action will yield results?

**5.3. Identify the next smallest steps to take with courage.**
- What are the smallest, most concrete actions you can take this week?

---

## Step 6 — Be Open to Change

Stay flexible as the situation evolves.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**6.1. Consider how to remain flexible as new facts emerge.**
- What would cause you to revise your plan?

**6.2. Release rigid expectations or perfectionist goals.**
- Where are you attached to a specific outcome?
- What would it look like to focus on the *process* instead of the *outcome*?

---

## Step 7 — Trust the Process

Recognize that contemplation is cyclical, and be gentle with yourself.

### Sub-questions to ask the user

**7.1. Reflect on what "enough" looks like in each moment.**
- 7.1.1. The process is cyclical and evolving — when will you revisit these steps?

**7.2. Be gentle to yourself.**
- You will make wrong decisions. You will never be perfect. That's okay.
- It's okay not to be perfect.

---

## Closing this framework — sanity check

Before finalizing the canvas, perform a sanity check:

- Does the resolution feel like it would hold up in three weeks?
- Is the next-smallest-step actually small enough to do this week?
- Is there something heavy that's been intellectualized rather than felt? Name it.

Confidence band on the conclusion: usually **medium** at best for contemplation work —
the situation is genuinely uncertain, that's why we're here. Mark it honestly.

Schedule a revisit. Contemplation is cyclical by design.

---

## Facilitation notes for this framework

- **Move slower than you think you should.** Contemplating is not a productivity exercise.
  If the user is rushing through the sub-questions, gently slow them down.
- **Don't rush past feelings.** Step 1.3 and 1.4 are not optional. Emotions distorting
  judgment is the entire reason this framework exists.
- **Don't problem-solve prematurely.** If you find yourself wanting to suggest solutions
  during Steps 1–3, you've shifted into Decision-Making or Problem-Solving framework. Name
  the shift and ask the user if they want to switch.
- **The canvas for this framework is private.** Unlike Decision or Design artifacts,
  contemplation outputs are usually for the user alone. Keep the language personal.
- **Visual models are usually not needed here.** Contemplating is mostly prose-shaped.
  The exceptions: a 2×2 of "what I can control / what I can't" against "what I have to do
  / what I want to do" can be useful at Step 3.
